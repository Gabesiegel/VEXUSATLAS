Your Practical Guide to Understanding VExUS

Introduction

Venous congestion, often linked to elevated right atrial pressure, is a significant clinical challenge. It can lead to organ damage, but identifying it through physical exams and lab tests can be tricky. That's where Point-of-Care Ultrasound (POCUS) comes in, and specifically, the Venous Excess Ultrasound (VExUS)Score protocol is gaining traction as a valuable bedside tool.

What is VExUS?

VExUS is a POCUS technique used to quantify venous congestion. It goes beyond simply looking at the Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) size. It combines IVC measurements with Doppler assessments of the:

  • Hepatic Vein (HV)
  • Portal Vein (PV)
  • Intrarenal Vein (IRV)

Why Use VExUS?

  • Non-invasive: Provides valuable hemodynamic information without invasive procedures.
  • Real-time assessment: Allows you to see the effects of interventions (like diuretics) immediately.
  • Guides therapy: Helps determine the severity of congestion and tailor treatment plans.
  • Potentially improves outcomes: Studies suggest a link between high VExUS scores and organ dysfunction.

Important Note: VExUS is a tool to enhance clinical judgment, not replace it. Always integrate your findings with the patient's history, physical exam, and other investigations.

Let's Get Started: A Step-by-Step Guide

1. Equipment and Preparation

  • Ultrasound Machine: Must have 2D imaging, color Doppler, and pulsed-wave Doppler capabilities.
  • Supplies: Ultrasound gel, gloves.
  • Patient Positioning: Supine position, table adjusted to the operator's waist level.

2. Assessing the Inferior Vena Cava (IVC)

    The IVC view, typically acquired using point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS), is critical for estimating right atrial pressure, as its diameter and degree of respiratory collapse reflect changes in central venous pressure. By measuring how the IVC responds during inspiration, clinicians can rapidly identify elevated venous pressures at the bedside. This is a foundational component of the VExUS score

VExUS Grading System

Grade IVC Diameter Hepatic Vein Flow Portal Vein Flow Intrarenal Vein Flow
0 ≤ 2 cm Normal S > D Normal Phasic Continuous
1 2–2.5 cm Slight Reduction in S Mild Pulsatility Slight Interruption
2 > 2.5 cm S < D / Reversal Marked Pulsatility Intermittent or Biphasic
3 > 2.5 cm + Minimal Collapse Severe Abnormal Flow To-and-Fro or Reversal Reversal

Conclusion

VExUS is a powerful POCUS technique for assessing venous congestion. By mastering the steps outlined above and understanding the potential pitfalls, you can incorporate VExUS into your practice to improve patient care and guide treatment decisions. Remember, practice and ongoing learning are key to becoming proficient in any POCUS application!
